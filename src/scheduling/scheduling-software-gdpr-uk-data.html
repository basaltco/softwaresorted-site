GDPR-Compliant Scheduling Software for UK Businesses (2026)

Every scheduling tool's own GDPR page tells you the same thing: trust us, we've got this covered. None of them tell you what you, as the business actually using the tool, are responsible for — and that gap is where most UK small businesses get caught out, usually only realising it when a client asks a pointed question about where their data is stored.

Quick Verdict

None of the four tools in this cluster are GDPR non-compliant — that's not really the right question. The real question is data residency and who holds responsibility for the paperwork. SimplyBook.me is EEA-headquartered (Iceland), giving it the most straightforward default position. Calendly, Acuity Scheduling, and YouCanBook.me are all effectively US-controlled and rely on Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) for lawful international transfer — usable, but you are responsible for having a Data Processing Addendum (DPA) in place, and none of these vendors will proactively remind you of that.

What "GDPR Compliant" Actually Means Here

A scheduling tool being "GDPR compliant" doesn't mean it's automatically fine to use for any kind of data. It means the vendor has the legal mechanisms in place to process personal data lawfully — but using those mechanisms correctly is partly your responsibility as the business collecting the data.

Two concepts matter for this comparison:

Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs). When a vendor stores or processes UK/EU customer data outside the UK/EEA (typically in the US), SCCs are the legal mechanism that makes this transfer lawful under UK GDPR. Calendly, Acuity, and YouCanBook.me all rely on SCCs because their infrastructure is US-controlled.

Data Processing Addendum (DPA). This is a contract between you (the business collecting client data) and the vendor, setting out how the vendor will process that data on your behalf. Most vendors make a DPA available, but you typically have to actively request or sign it — it's not automatically in place just because you've signed up for the tool.

Who Should Care Most About This

This matters more for some businesses than others. If you're a freelance graphic designer booking discovery calls, the data you collect through a booking page (name, email, project type) is low-sensitivity, and any of the four tools is a reasonable choice with a DPA in place.

If you're a therapist, counsellor, medical-adjacent practitioner, or any business collecting health, financial, or otherwise special-category data at the point of booking, the ICO treats this as higher-risk processing. In that case:

  • Read the vendor's DPA in full before collecting any client data through the tool, not after.
  • Consider SimplyBook.me's EEA headquarters as a structurally simpler starting point, all else being equal.
  • If you choose a US-controlled tool (Calendly, Acuity, YouCanBook.me), confirm the SCCs and DPA are genuinely in place for your account, not just published as a general policy.

What to Actually Do Before You Commit

  1. Check whether the tool's headquarters and data storage location matter for your specific use case — they matter more for sensitive-data businesses than for general bookings.
  2. Locate the vendor's DPA (usually linked from their privacy or legal page) and confirm what it covers.
  3. If you're collecting anything beyond basic contact and scheduling information, document why you're satisfied the tool meets your obligations — this is good practice even if no one ever asks.
  4. Re-check this annually or after any change of ownership at the vendor (as happened with YouCanBook.me's 2025 acquisition) — GDPR posture can change without an obvious announcement.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a Data Processing Addendum (DPA) to use Calendly in the UK?

If you're collecting any personal data from clients through your Calendly booking page — which you are, even just name and email — yes, a DPA should be in place. Calendly publishes one, but you're responsible for reviewing and accepting it; it isn't automatically applied just by signing up.

Which scheduling tool stores data in the UK or EEA?

SimplyBook.me, headquartered in Iceland (EEA), has the most straightforward data residency position of the four. Calendly, Acuity, and YouCanBook.me are all effectively US-controlled and rely on Standard Contractual Clauses.

What's the difference between GDPR compliance and data residency?

GDPR compliance means the legal mechanisms (like SCCs and a DPA) are in place to process data lawfully, regardless of where it's stored. Data residency refers specifically to the physical/legal location where the data is stored and processed. A tool can be GDPR compliant without having UK/EEA data residency, as long as the transfer mechanisms are correctly in place.

Do I need extra safeguards if I'm a therapist or healthcare-adjacent business?

Yes. The ICO treats health and similar special-category data as higher-risk processing. Read the vendor's DPA in full, confirm SCCs are in place if using a US-controlled tool, and consider whether an EEA-headquartered option like SimplyBook.me reduces complexity for your specific situation.

What are Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) and why do they matter?

SCCs are a legal mechanism that makes it lawful to transfer personal data from the UK/EEA to a country without equivalent data protection law, such as the US. Calendly, Acuity, and YouCanBook.me all rely on SCCs because their data processing happens on US-controlled infrastructure.

Which scheduling tool is the safest default for GDPR-sensitive use cases?

SimplyBook.me, due to its EEA headquarters, removes one layer of complexity (no SCC dependency for EEA-to-EEA processing). It isn't the only valid choice — the US-based tools are lawful to use with a proper DPA — but it requires less active diligence from you to confirm.
